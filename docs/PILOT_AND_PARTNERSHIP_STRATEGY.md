# NAMA Pilot and Partnership Strategy

**Version:** 1.0  
**Status:** Documentation Only — Pilot Design, Partner Selection, and KPI Framework  
**Project:** NAMA — Global Trust Infrastructure for Sustainable Food Systems

> ⚠️ **Non-binding disclaimer:** This document is a strategic planning reference for pilot design, partner selection, KPI tracking, and commercial validation. All pilot structures, thresholds, KPI ranges, and partnership models are illustrative and must be adapted to the legal, operational, and regulatory context of each real-world pilot. Nothing in this document modifies smart contracts, API logic, indexer logic, frontend logic, deployment workflows, docker-compose, or any on-chain financial mechanism.

---

## 1. Strategic Principle

NAMA should begin with a **narrow, measurable pilot**, but not as a generic traceability tool. The objective is to prove operational value in a constrained environment while preserving NAMA's differentiated sustainability and procurement model.

The pilot should position NAMA as:

- **Trusted food DPP infrastructure** for product-level provenance, verification, and auditability
- **AII-based value recovery layer** that converts natural imperfection into procurement and recovery intelligence
- **Circular procurement engine** that routes value into secondary and responsible use cases
- **ESG proof layer** that turns verified events into credible institutional reporting evidence
- **Institutional procurement intelligence platform** that links traceability, operational efficiency, and measurable ROI

This means the pilot should demonstrate not only that NAMA can record traceability data, but that it can turn trusted data into **procurement, circularity, sustainability, and commercial proof**.

---

## 2. Pilot Philosophy

NAMA's first pilots should follow a disciplined execution model:

- Start with **one use case**
- Start in **one geography or operational site**
- Work with **one or two institutional partners**
- Define **measurable 3–9 month outcomes**
- Avoid broad multi-sector scope at the beginning
- Prove ROI before expanding
- Optimize for operational proof before ecosystem expansion

The first pilot should be small enough to manage closely, but substantial enough to generate credible evidence. Early success should come from a clear operational improvement, a measurable sustainability result, or a validated commercial model — not from ecosystem scale claims.

## 3. Recommended First Pilot Models

### A. Institutional AII Procurement Pilot

**Candidate participants**

- Hospitals
- Airlines
- Cloud kitchens
- Large kitchens

**Purpose**

- Procure imperfect but safe produce using AII
- Measure cost savings
- Issue ESG procurement badges

**Strategic rationale**

This pilot is designed to show that NAMA can turn trusted product data and AII classification into better institutional buying decisions. It is especially suitable where food budgets, supply consistency, and sustainability reporting all matter.

**Primary proof goals**

- Demonstrate institutional acceptance of AII-qualified produce
- Compare procurement costs against conventional sourcing baseline
- Prove repeat purchasing behavior if quality and pricing hold
- Generate verifiable procurement-linked ESG evidence

### B. Municipal Circular Recovery Pilot

**Candidate participants**

- Municipality
- Central market
- Food recovery partner
- Animal feed or circular recovery partner

**Purpose**

- Divert food surplus or non-retail food into secondary value chains
- Measure kg diverted
- Calculate revenue share
- Issue circular ESG badges

**Strategic rationale**

This pilot is designed to validate NAMA's ability to convert traceability and routing intelligence into measurable circular value. It is especially relevant where municipal waste reduction and partner accountability are visible policy priorities.

**Primary proof goals**

- Track recoverable surplus moving into verified secondary channels
- Quantify diversion and recovery outcomes
- Validate whether a revenue-share structure can be operationally measured
- Produce credible circularity evidence for public or institutional stakeholders

### C. Cold-Chain DPP Pilot

**Candidate participants**

- Seafood
- Fresh produce
- Cold-chain logistics

**Purpose**

- Use DPP + IoT telemetry to prove origin, temperature integrity, and compliance

**Strategic rationale**

This pilot is designed to prove that NAMA can anchor high-trust, product-level evidence for sensitive food categories where origin, cold-chain integrity, and compliance are critical.

**Primary proof goals**

- Show end-to-end DPP creation and verification for selected batches
- Measure whether telemetry improves confidence in transport integrity
- Demonstrate faster access to batch history and origin evidence
- Support compliance-oriented reporting without broad ecosystem complexity

## 4. Partner Selection Criteria

Pilot partners should be selected for measurable execution value, not just visibility.

### Preferred attributes

- Government or regulatory partner preferred but not mandatory
- Institutional buyer with measurable procurement flows
- Local IoT or cold-chain partner
- Supplier or cooperative willing to participate in data capture

### Protection filters

- Avoid early dependency on dominant monopolistic logistics partners
- Avoid partners demanding ownership of NAMA's data model or source code

### Additional screening considerations

- Ability to provide baseline operational data
- Willingness to define a limited pilot scope
- Internal operator capacity for training and reporting
- Commercial openness to KPI-based renewal rather than indefinite experimentation

## 5. KPI Framework

The KPI framework should prove business, operational, sustainability, and trust value. KPIs should be selected per pilot, but the categories below provide the default framework.

### A. Traceability KPIs

| KPI | Purpose |
|-----|---------|
| Number of DPPs created | Measures product-level traceability coverage |
| Average time to verify product origin | Measures verification speed for buyers, auditors, or inspectors |
| Average time to retrieve batch history | Measures audit and investigation efficiency |
| Percentage of batches with complete DPP data | Measures passport completeness |
| Number of QR/DPP verification events | Measures actual verification usage |
| Number of verified certification events | Measures trusted compliance and certification evidence |

### B. Procurement KPIs

| KPI | Purpose |
|-----|---------|
| Number of B2B procurement transactions | Measures transactional use |
| Total value of institutional procurement routed through NAMA | Measures pilot commercial throughput |
| Number of AII-qualified batches purchased | Measures use of the AII model |
| Buyer repeat procurement rate | Measures adoption quality and repeat confidence |
| Average procurement cost reduction versus conventional sourcing | Measures buyer ROI against baseline |
| Verified savings generated | Measures validated economic value |
| Percentage of procurement using imperfect but safe produce | Measures value recovery through procurement decisions |

### C. Circular Economy KPIs

| KPI | Purpose |
|-----|---------|
| kg of food waste diverted | Measures physical diversion outcome |
| Number of circular routes completed | Measures operational execution volume |
| Circular recovery revenue generated | Measures value created in secondary channels |
| NAMA revenue share from circular recovery | Measures platform-side commercial validation |
| Municipality or recovery partner revenue share | Measures partner-side economic participation |
| Partner institution revenue share | Measures incentive alignment for participating institutions |
| Number of circular ESG badges issued | Measures proof generation linked to circular impact |

### D. ESG / Sustainability KPIs

| KPI | Purpose |
|-----|---------|

| Estimated avoided waste | Measures non-disposed food or recovered value |

### E. Operational Efficiency KPIs

| KPI | Purpose |

| KPI | Purpose |
|-----|---------|

### H. Commercial ROI KPIs

| KPI | Purpose |
|-----|---------|

## 6. KPI Measurement Plan

KPI measurement should follow a disciplined cadence:

1. **Baseline measurement before pilot**  
   Document the existing operational process, manual verification time, procurement cost baseline, reporting burden, and current diversion or recovery outcomes before the pilot starts.

2. **Weekly or monthly KPI tracking**  
   Track the selected KPIs at a fixed cadence appropriate to transaction volume and partner capacity.

3. **Pilot midpoint review**  
   Review KPI progress, identify operational bottlenecks, confirm data quality, and decide whether any KPI definitions need clarification.

4. **Final pilot impact report**  
   Produce a structured summary of measured outcomes, lessons learned, commercial implications, and next-step recommendations.

5. **Recommended pilot duration: 3–9 months**  
   Shorter periods may be insufficient for repeat procurement or circular routing patterns. Longer periods should require explicit justification.

6. **KPI-based renewal decision**  
   Renewal or expansion should depend on measured performance, not narrative enthusiasm.

### Measurement principles

- Separate operational KPIs from financial KPIs
- Avoid claiming impact that is not measured
- Use the same calculation method throughout the pilot
- Document assumptions for any estimates such as avoided emissions
- Keep baseline and pilot-period data clearly distinguishable

## 7. Pilot Success Thresholds

The following thresholds are **illustrative, non-binding examples only**. They are not legal promises, guaranteed outcomes, or universal targets. Each pilot should customize success thresholds to its scope, baseline, and partner context.

### Example threshold directions

- DPP completeness improves over the pilot period
- Traceability verification time is reduced compared to the manual process
- Measurable kg of food diverted is reported
- At least one institutional buyer repeats procurement
- At least one ESG or circular impact report is generated
- At least one savings or revenue-share model is validated
- No partner receives exclusive control over NAMA data or source code

### Example range logic

- **Traceability completeness:** meaningful improvement from baseline toward high-confidence operational coverage
- **Verification speed:** measurable reduction from baseline manual verification time
- **Procurement repetition:** at least one repeat order or repeat sourcing cycle by a participating buyer
- **Circular proof:** at least one complete route documented with evidence and reporting output
- **Commercial proof:** at least one validated savings case or one validated recovery revenue case

These thresholds should be adapted before each pilot and approved as part of the pilot scope document.

## 8. Commercial Validation

The KPI framework should support NAMA's commercial operating model by proving business value before scale.

### Shared Savings Procurement

Pilot data should show whether AII-qualified sourcing can produce measurable buyer savings compared with conventional procurement.

### Circular Recovery Revenue Share

Pilot data should show whether surplus routing and recovery flows can generate measurable value that supports a revenue-share model.

### Strategic Supplier Participation

Pilot data should show whether suppliers that participate consistently create stronger trust, data quality, and procurement reliability outcomes.

### Commercial principle

The pilot should prove business value first, not token economics. Commercial validation should come from verified savings, recovery value, operational efficiency, and repeat partner behavior.

## 9. Partnership Protection Principles

Every pilot agreement should preserve NAMA's strategic and intellectual independence.

- No source code transfer
- No unrestricted historical data access
- No exclusive lock-in during pilot
- Defined pilot scope
- Defined pilot duration
- KPI-based renewal
- IP and data protection clauses
- Anti-copycat protections
- No partner right to replicate the platform without agreement

These protections help ensure that pilot partners can validate real-world value without absorbing or controlling the core platform.

---

## 10. Red Flags

The following conditions should trigger caution, scope reduction, or a decision not to proceed:

- Partner demanding exclusivity too early
- Partner demanding full data ownership
- Partner demanding source code
- Partner pushing token-first economics before ROI is proven
- Pilot scope expanding too quickly
- Dominant logistics player trying to control the platform
- Unclear buyer ROI
- Refusal to define measurable KPIs
- Refusal to share operational baseline data for comparison

If multiple red flags appear simultaneously, NAMA should prefer delaying the pilot over entering a structurally weak partnership.

## 11. Lessons from Global Market

Comparable market examples suggest several consistent lessons:

- Government or regulatory adoption often helps operational traceability pilots gain legitimacy, coordination, and implementation discipline
- Enterprise traceability tends to succeed when linked to concrete operational value such as compliance efficiency, procurement optimization, or verification speed
- Sector-specific provenance models generally perform better than generic overbroad launches because the workflow, incentives, and reporting needs are clearer
- Many blockchain supply-chain pilots fail when they lack ROI, a realistic integration path, or committed partner adoption

These lessons should guide strategy, but they should not be treated as proof of NAMA's success. NAMA should learn from comparable examples without claiming equivalence to IBM, TE-FOOD, Everledger, or any other platform.

---

## 12. NAMA Differentiation

NAMA is differentiated not simply by using traceability infrastructure, but by linking traceability to procurement, circularity, and institutional proof.

- **AII** converts natural imperfection into procurement intelligence
- **Circular Procurement** converts waste or diversion into measurable value
- **DPP** creates product-level trust anchors
- **ESG badges** convert operational impact into institutional proof
- **Dual Interface** separates enterprise and producer experiences
- **Commercial Model** links technology to savings and revenue sharing
- **KPI framework** turns sustainability claims into measurable pilot evidence

This means NAMA should not be positioned as only a provenance platform. Its distinct value comes from making verified operational data commercially and institutionally usable.
